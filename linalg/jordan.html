  • Jordan Decomposition

  • Given a linear operator \(T\) on an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\), which characteristic polynomial splits. Let \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_r\) be the eigenvalues and \(b_1, \ldots, b_r\) the corresponding algebraic multiplicities. Then we have \[V=\oplus^r_{i=1} N((A-\lambda_i I)^{b_i})\] and for each \(i\) there exists a basis for \( N((A-\lambda_i I)^{b_i})\) in the form \[(T-\lambda_iI)^{b_i-1} v_1, (T-\lambda_iI)^{b_i-2} v_1, \ldots, v_1, (T-\lambda_iI)^{b_i-1} v_2, \ldots, v_2, \ldots, (T-\lambda_iI)^{b_i-1}v_s, \ldots, v_s.\]
  • Rough idea of the proof:
  • First we claim that the decomposition holds: when there is only one eigenvalue, let \(p\) be the characteristic polynomial of \(T\), then we have \(p(T)=(T-\lambda_1)^n=0\) by the Cayley-Hamilton theorem. This implies \[V\subset N((T-\lambda_1)^n)\subset V.\] We can always decompose \[V=N((T-\lambda_1)^{b_1})\oplus R((T-\lambda_1)^{b_1})\] where the range \(R((T-\lambda_1)^{b_1})\) has dimension strictly less than \(n\). The decomposition follows by induction.
  • Next we claim the construction of basis. Fix \(i\) and omit \(i\) in the following argument. We do this by induction on \(n=dim V\). When \(n=1\) any linear map can either be trivial or scaling, and we can pick a nontrivial eigenvector to be the basis. This closes the base case. Suppose we can find such basis on any \(V\) of dimension less than \(n\). Decompose \[V=N(T-\lambda I)\oplus R(T-\lambda I)\] then we can find a basis for \(R(T-\lambda I)\) in the form \[v_1, (T-\lambda_iI)v_1, \ldots, (T-\lambda_iI)^{b_i-1} v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_s,\ldots, (T-\lambda_iI)^{b_i-1}v_s.\] and write them into \[u_1, \ldots, u_s, (T-\lambda I)u_1, \ldots, (T-\lambda I)u_s,\ldots, (T-\lambda I)^b u_1, \ldots, (T-\lambda I)^b u_s\] for some \(u_1, \ldots, u_s\in V\). They are linearly independent. Extend the list to a basis of \(V\) by introducing some \[w_1, \ldots, w_p.\] \((T-\lambda)w_1,\ldots, (T-\lambda )w_p\) lies in the range \(R(T-\lambda I)\) so for each \(w_j\) we can write it into a linear combination of \((T-\lambda I)u_1, \ldots, (T-\lambda I)u_s,\ldots, (T-\lambda I)^b u_1, \ldots, (T-\lambda I)^b u_s\). In other words, there exists a unique \(x_j\in R(T-\lambda I)\) such that \[Nw_j=Nx_j.\] Replace each \(w_j\) by \(w_j-x_j\) then we obtain a desired basis. The result follows by induction.
  • Application:
  • Given a matrix \(A\), we can write it into \[A=PBP^{-1}\] where \(B\) is a block matrix and the columns of \(P\) form a basis as above.
