  • General picture on the relation between: linear system, vector space and determinant

  • Given a matrix \(A=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1&3\\2&4\end{array}\right)\) we can visualize it as follows:
  • The vector \(\vec{a}\) is the first column of \(A\) and \(\vec{b}\) is the second column of \(A\).
  • The determinant of \(A\) is the signed area of the bounded parallelogram formed by \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\). A positive sign indicates positive orientation (same orientation as xy-plane) and a negative sign indicates the opposite orientation.
  • The determinant is non-zero as long as the two vectors do not degenerate into a single line; i.e. they are linearly independent.
  • As shown, the vectors \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\) are pointing at different and non-opposite directions. Suppose \(p\vec{a} =q\vec{b}\) for some scalars \(p, q\). Scalar multiplication is just scaling (and probably revesing a direction), \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\) are pointing to different directions and any scaling cannot change this fact, unless we ``cheat" with the definition and multiply by 0 for both \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\).
  • In other words, the linear system \(LS(A, 0)\) has unique and trivial solution.
  • We say that the null space \(N(A)=0\) and the column space \(C(A)=\mathbb{R}^2\).
  • This satisfies the rank-nullity theorem by \(0+2=2\).
  • By row operations, we have \[A \xrightarrow{-2R_1+R_2} \left(\begin{array}{cc}1&3\\0&-2\end{array}\right) \xrightarrow{-\frac{1}{2}R_2}\left(\begin{array}{cc}1&3\\0&1\end{array}\right) \xrightarrow{-3R_2+R_1}\left(\begin{array}{cc}1&0\\0&1\end{array}\right)\] which has 2 pivot columns (corresponds to the rank, i.e. the dimension of column space) and 0 free columns (corresponds to the nullity, i.e. the dimension of null space).
  • Column operations on \(A\) does not change the column space, because after any operations, the columns we get are linear combinations of \(\vec{a}, \vec{b}\).
  • Row operationss on \(A\) does not change the column space, because saying there exists \(\vec{v}\) lying in \(C(A)\), the column space of \(A\), is equivalent to saying that \(LS(A,\vec{v})\) has unique non-trivial solution. Row operation on \(A\) does not change this fact.
  • Alternatively, we can interpret \(A\) as the linear transformation on \(\mathbb{R}^2\) determined by \[e_1\mapsto \vec{a};\hspace{2cm} e_2 \mapsto \vec{b}\] This is because \(Ae_1=\vec{a}\) and \(Ae_2=\vec{b}\).
  • This is actually a better way to read linear algebra.
  • In this way we say that \[vol(A(Q))=det(A(Q))=A^*vol(Q)\] where \(Q\) is the unit square bounded by \(e_1, e_2\) and \(A^*\) means pulling back measure by \(A\).
  • Volume here is actually area. But the philosophy can be extended to higher dimensions.
