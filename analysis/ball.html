Volume of \(n\)-ball

  • The volume of \(n\)-dimensional ball with radius 1 is \[\alpha(n)=\frac{\pi^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\Gamma(\frac{1}{2}n+1)}.\] For convenience we denote the solid ball by \(\mathbb{B}^n\) and its boundary by \(\partial \mathbb{B}^n=\mathbb{S}^{n-1}\).

  • Proof.

  • We parametrize the surface By \[f(r, \theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_{n-1})=(r\cos \theta_1, r\sin \theta_1 \cos \theta_2, r\sin \theta_1 \sin \theta_2 \cos \theta_3, \ldots, r\sin \theta_1 \sin \theta_{n-2}\sin \theta_{n-1})\] where \(r\in [0,1], \theta_1\in [0,2\pi), \theta_2,\ldots, \theta_{n-1}\in [0,\pi)\). The metric, or the first fundamental form, is given by \[g_{\mathbb{B}^n}=\left[ \begin{array}{ccccc}1&&&&\\ &r^2\sin^2 \theta_1&&&\\ &&r^2\sin^2\theta_1 \sin^2\theta_2&&\\ &&&\ddots&\\ &&&& r^2\sin^2 \theta_1 \sin^2\theta_2 \ldots \sin^2 \theta_{n-2} \end{array}\right]\]
    • where \(g_{ij}=f_i\cdot f_j\) for \(i,j=r, \theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_{n-1}\), and hence the volume of \(\mathbb{B}^n\) is \[ \begin{align} |\mathbb{B}^n|&=\int^{2\pi}_0\int^\pi_0\ldots\int^\pi_0\int^1_0 r^nsin^{n-2}\theta_1 sin^{n-3}\theta_2\ldots\sin \theta_{n-2}\mathrm{d}r\mathrm{d}\theta \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{n}\prod^{n-2}_{m=1}\int^\pi_0\sin^m \theta\mathrm{d}\theta \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{n}\int^\pi_0\sin^{n-2}\theta\mathrm{d}\theta \int^\pi_0\sin^{n-3}\theta\mathrm{d}\theta \prod^{n-4}_{m=1}\int^\pi_0\sin^m \theta\mathrm{d}\theta \\ &=\frac{2\pi}{n}\int^\pi_0\sin^{n-2}\theta\mathrm{d}\theta \int^\pi_0\sin^{n-3}\theta\mathrm{d}\theta \frac{n-2}{2\pi}|\mathbb{B}^{n-2}|\\ &= \frac{n-2}{n}\left(\frac{n-3}{n-2}\frac{n-4}{n-3}\ldots\frac{1}{2}\pi\right) \left(\frac{n-4}{n-3}\frac{n-5}{n-4}\ldots\frac{2}{3}2\right)|\mathbb{B}^{n-2}|\\ &=\frac{2\pi}{n}|\mathbb{B}^{n-2}| \end{align} \] The same recurrence relation is satisfied by \[\alpha(n)=\frac{\pi^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\Gamma(\frac{n}{2}+1)}\] where \(\Gamma(s)=\int^\infty_0 t^{s-1}e^{-t}\mathrm{d} t \), so we have \[|\mathbb{B}^n|=\alpha(n).\]
We denote \(\omega(n)=|\mathbb{S}^{n-1}\subset \mathbb{R}^n|=|\partial \mathbb{B}^n|=n\alpha(n)\) and this is derived from scaling and differentiation.
