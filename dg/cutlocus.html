Cut locus and Conjugate points
  • Let \( (M,g) \) be a Riemannian manifold and \( p\) be a point. The cut locus of \( p\) is the collection overflow cut points, i.e. points \(q\) that are either (i) conjugate to \(p\) along some geodesic, or (ii) there exists two distinct geodesics joining \(p, q\) with the same length.
  • \(q\) is a conujugate point of \(p\) along a geodesic \(\gamma\) if there exists a Jacobi field \( J\) such that \(J(0)=J(1)=0\) for \(\gamma(0)=p, \gamma(1)=q\).
  • Claim: The cut locus of any point \(p\) in \( \mathbb{RP}^1=\mathbb{S}^2/ \mathbb{Z}^2\) is a circle, with conjugate point \(\{p\}\) only.
  • Proof: Let \(p\) be the North pole. Draw a geodesic down to the equator at a point \(q\). \(q\) is identified with the point \(q'\) opposite to \(q\) itself on the equator. The equator is the cut locus and the injectivity radius is \(\frac{\pi}{2}\).
  • Claim: Cut locus of \(\mathbb{RP}^n, \mathbb{CP}^n\) are \(\mathbb{RP}^{n-1}, \mathbb{CP}^{n-1}\) respectively.
  • Claim: Cut locus of \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) cannot be \(\mathbb{S}^1\).
  • Proof: By definition of cut locus, \(M-C(p)\) is contractible to a point and hence give an \(n\)-cell. By Mayer-Vietoris theorem for cellular homology, compare \(H_2(\mathbb{CP}^2)\) obtained by considering \(\mathbb{CP}^2=\mathbb{C} \cup \mathbb{CP}^1\) and \(\mathbb{CP}^2=\mathbb{S}^1\cup D^4\) where \(D^4\) is the 4-cell (4-dimensional closed ball in \( \mathbb{R}^4\) ).
